New Structural and Arithmetic Constraints on Finite-Support Periodic Highways of Langton’s Ant

Atharva Jillhewar
Preprint — 2026  ·  LaTeX source  ·  project index
Scope. Every result below is a necessary condition on a finite-support periodic highway, or an exact finite computation. None asserts that any orbit converges, so none resolves the (open) highway conjecture. The aim is an arithmetic/topological theory of what a classical finite-support highway can look like.
Abstract. For the classical two-colour Langton’s ant on \(\Z^2\) with finite initial black support, we establish structural and arithmetic constraints on the possible periodic highways. (1) An even-winding theorem: no finite-support pattern can drift to an exact translate of itself with restored heading and zero net change in the number of black cells; consequently every finite-support periodic highway must build a wake whose net black growth is a strictly positive multiple of four. (2) A signed mod-four wake-residue identity: the checkerboard-signed strand bases sum to \(2\pmod 4\) in each residue class of every nonzero drift coordinate, whence the lower bound \(\growth\ge 2\max(|a|,|b|)\). (3) A Tait-graph conjugacy with a cycle-rank surgery identity \(E+\Delta C=2\beta\). (4) A two-endpoint collision-chain parity theorem (and, building on Traldi’s interlacement theory, a touch-graph rank computation for the periodic trace). (5) A two-engine, rank-audited exhaustive computation excluding every finite-support periodic highway of nonzero drift and period at most \(48\), so any nonstandard periodic highway must have even period at least \(50\). We also give a decidable periodic-realisability criterion with an explicit finite seed. The algebraic core is machine-checked in Lean 4. Every result is a necessary condition on a finite-support periodic highway or an exact finite computation; none resolves the (open) convergence conjecture, and we delimit precisely what a solution must still supply.

1. Introduction

Langton’s ant is the turmite on the square lattice \(\Z^2\): on a white cell it turns \(90^\circ\) right, on a black cell \(90^\circ\) left; it then flips the current cell’s colour and moves one unit forward. Started on the all-white plane it produces, after a transient of \(9977\) steps, a self-similar diagonal highway of least period \(104\) that translates the ant by \((\pm2,\pm2)\) each period. We impose finite support: only finitely many cells are initially black.

Conjecture 1.1 (Highway conjecture). For every finite initial black set and every initial pose, the orbit of Langton’s ant eventually enters a translate, rotation, reflection, and phase shift of the standard period-\(104\) highway.

1.1. Prior art

Every trajectory is unbounded (Bunimovich–Troubetzkoy [1]; the checkerboard/parity structure is in Gale–Propp–Sutherland–Troubetzkoy [6]). Prediction problems for the ant are computationally universal and undecidable, so no finite time cutoff can settle Conjecture 1.1 [4]. Gajardo [5] characterises the forbidden factors of the ant’s trace subshift. Recent progress concerns generalised ants: some admit infinitely many highways [3], others have non-highway behaviour [7], and confinement bounds are known [2]. We are not aware of prior results constraining what a classical finite-support periodic highway can look like; this paper aims at that layer.

1.2. Results

A repeating trace is an infinite turn word \(w^\omega\) realised by a finite-support colouring (§3); its drift \(\drift=(a,b)\) is the per-period displacement and its growth \(\growth\) the per-period change in the number of black cells.

  1. Even-winding theorem (Theorem 6.1): no finite-support clean translator with nonzero drift exists; hence every periodic highway has \(\growth\in4\Z_{>0}\) (Corollary 6.5). Bunimovich–Troubetzkoy excludes only bounded repeatable orbits; a zero-growth glider is unbounded, so the strict positivity is new.
  2. Signed mod-four wake-residue identity (Theorem 7.1) and the lower bound \(\growth\ge2\max(|a|,|b|)\) (Corollary 7.3). No exact per-residue identity or linear drift–growth bound appears in the literature.
  3. Tait-graph conjugacy (Theorem 4.1) and cycle-rank surgery \(E+\Delta C=2\beta\) (Theorem 4.2). We are not aware of prior Langton-ant work using medial/Tait graphs.
  4. Collision-chain parity (Theorem 8.1). We additionally record, building on Traldi’s circuit-partition/interlacement theory [9],[8], a rank computation for the periodic touch graph (Observation 8.2); a self-contained treatment is deferred to the companion report.
  5. Small-period exclusion (Theorem 9.1): a two-engine, rank-audited search excludes every finite-support periodic highway of nonzero drift and period at most \(48\).

Section 3 also gives a decidable periodic-realisability criterion with an explicit finite seed, strengthening the forbidden-word program of [5]; only that strengthening is claimed as new. The heading/black-count invariant (Lemma 2.4) is folklore [1],[6].

1.3. Scope

Every theorem is a necessary condition on a finite-support periodic highway, or an exact finite computation; none forces convergence, so none resolves Conjecture 1.1. The missing geometric ingredient is stated in §10.

2. Setup and elementary facts

A state is \((B,p,q)\) with \(B\subset\Z^2\) finite, \(p\in\Z^2\), and heading \(q\in\Z/4\Z\) (\(0=N,1=E,2=S,3=W\)), with unit vectors \(u_0=(0,1),u_1=(1,0),u_2=(0,-1),u_3=(-1,0)\). The turn is \(\turnR\) (\(\varepsilon=+1\)) if \(p\notin B\) and \(\turnL\) (\(\varepsilon=-1\)) if \(p\in B\); one step is \begin{equation}\label{eq:step} s=(B,p,q)\ \longmapsto\ \bigl(B\,\triangle\,\{p\},\ p+u_{q+\varepsilon},\ q+\varepsilon\bigr). \end{equation} The dynamics is reversible. Write \(b_t=|B_t|\) and \(\che(x,y)=(-1)^{x+y}\).

Proposition 2.1 (Checkerboard/HV partition [6]). The quantity \(\bigl(q_t-(p_{t,x}+p_{t,y})\bigr)\bmod 2\) is constant along every orbit. Hence each cell is entered always horizontally or always vertically; calling a cell vertical or horizontal accordingly, at a vertical cell the two vertical edges are the only admissible arrival edges and the horizontal ones the only departures, and conversely. Every admissible directed edge carries a fixed orientation.
Proof. Each step changes the heading by an odd quarter-turn, so \(q_t\bmod2\) alternates; \(u_{q_{t+1}}\) has odd \(\ell^1\)-length, so \((p_{t,x}+p_{t,y})\bmod2\) alternates; their difference is invariant. At a fixed cell the checkerboard parity is constant, so the arrival axis is fixed. Edge orientations follow since an arrival edge of one endpoint is a departure edge of the other.
Proposition 2.2 (Alternating visits). At any fixed cell the encountered turns alternate strictly. All but finitely many cells begin with \(\turnR\).
Proof. Each visit flips the colour and the turn is a function of colour, so the turn alternates; a cell white at first visit begins with \(\turnR\), and only the finitely many initially black cells begin with \(\turnL\).
Theorem 2.3 (Unboundedness [1]). Every finite-support orbit visits infinitely many cells.
Proof sketch. Were the visited set finite, reversibility would make the orbit periodic. A visited cell \(z\) maximising \(x+y\) has unvisited north and east neighbours; if \(z\) is horizontal it is entered from the west, and its two successive visits leave south then north, forcing a visit to an unvisited corner neighbour—a contradiction; the vertical case is the rotation.
Lemma 2.4 (Heading/black-count invariant; folklore). For every \(t\), \begin{equation}\label{eq:invariant} q_t-b_t\equiv q_0-b_0\pmod 4,\qquad p_{t+1}-p_t=u_{\,q_0-b_0+b_{t+1}} . \end{equation} A heading-resetting period changes \(b\) by a multiple of \(4\).
Proof. A white/\(\turnR\) step increments both \(q\) and \(b\); a black/\(\turnL\) step decrements both. So \(q_t-b_t\) is constant, and since the move follows the turn, \(q_{t+1}\equiv q_0-b_0+b_{t+1}\).

3. A constructive periodic-realisability criterion

Fix \(q_0\) and a turn word \(w=w_0\cdots w_{N-1}\); compute its path \(p_0,\dots,p_N\) (Proposition 2.1). Assume \(q_N=q_0\) and \(\drift=p_N-p_0\neq0\). Group phases by \(i\sim j\iff p_i-p_j\in\Z\drift\); in class \(I\) write \(p_i=b_I+a_i\drift\), and for \(n\ge\min_I a_i\) let \(S_{I,n}\) be the subword of all \(w_i\) (\(i\in I\)) with \(a_i\le n\), ordered by the key \((n-a_i,\,i)\).

Theorem 3.1 (Periodic-realisability criterion). \(w^\omega\) is the trace of a finite-support colouring if and only if, for every class \(I\): (i) every \(S_{I,n}\) with \(\min_I a_i\le n\le\max_I a_i\) alternates strictly, and (ii) the stabilised word \(S_{I,\max_I a_i}\) begins with \(\turnR\). Then an explicit finite black seed is \begin{equation}\label{eq:seed} B=\bigl\{\,b_I+n\drift:\ \textstyle\min_I a_i\le n<\max_I a_i\ \text{and}\ S_{I,n}\ \text{begins with}\ \turnL\,\bigr\}. \end{equation}
Proof. The occurrence of phase \(i\in I\) in period \(k\ge0\) sits at \(b_I+(a_i+k)\drift\), equal to \(b_I+n\drift\) iff \(k=n-a_i\ge0\); chronological order is lexicographic in \((n-a_i,i)\). So \(S_{I,n}\) is the chronological turn sequence at \(b_I+n\drift\) under \(w^\omega\).
Necessity. Each visit flips the cell, giving (i). For \(n\ge\max_I a_i\) all phases are present and the order is fixed; the infinitely many cells \(b_I+n\drift\) share this first turn, which by Proposition 2.2 must be \(\turnR\), giving (ii).
Sufficiency. Colour \(B\) black. Each boundary cell reproduces the first symbol of \(S_{I,n}\), and alternation supplies the rest; each stabilised cell begins with \(\turnR\), matching white. Induction on time gives \(w^\omega\) forever; the position advances by \(\drift\neq0\) each period.
Remark 3.2. Equivalently, sort each class by decreasing \(a_i\) (ties by increasing \(i\)); every \(S_{I,n}\) is a suffix, so it suffices to check the one stabilised word. This strengthens the forbidden-word characterisation [5] to a decidable global periodic-realisability test with a constructive seed. A worked instance is in Appendix A.

4. The Tait-graph conjugacy

Normalise so even cells receive vertical arrivals. The frozen all-white routing consists of clockwise four-cycles around one checkerboard class of unit plaquettes; take these as vertices of a rotated square lattice. Each ant cell \(z=(x,y)\) corresponds to the Tait edge \begin{equation}\label{eq:tait} e_z=\begin{cases}\{(x,y-1),(x-1,y)\}, & x+y\ \text{even},\\ \{(x-1,y-1),(x,y)\}, & x+y\ \text{odd},\end{cases} \end{equation} and \(H_B=\{e_z:z\in B\}\) is the finite black edge subgraph.

Theorem 4.1 (Boundary-loop conjugacy). White and black at \(z\) are the two smoothings of the directed crossing. Hence the frozen routing loops are the boundary components of a regular neighbourhood of \(H_B\) together with untouched four-cycles; the ant traverses its current boundary loop, toggles \(e_z\), and each step merges two loops or splits one. This is an exact conjugacy.
Theorem 4.2 (Cycle-rank surgery identity). With \(E=|B|\), \(V\) incident vertices, \(k\) components, and \(\beta=E-V+k\), a regular neighbourhood of \(H_B\) has \(k+\beta\) boundary components; relative to the \(V\) trivial loops, \(\Delta C=E-2V+2k\) and \(E+\Delta C=2\beta\). Adding a bridge merges loops with \(\beta\) fixed; adding a cycle edge splits a loop and raises \(\beta\) by one; removing a bridge splits with \(\beta\) fixed; removing a non-bridge merges and lowers \(\beta\) by one.
Proof. A regular neighbourhood of a graph is a surface of Euler characteristic \(V-E\) with \(k\) pieces, whose boundary has \(k+\beta\) circles (\(\beta=E-V+k\)). The identities are the difference from the trivial loops; the surgery cases are the standard bridge/cycle-edge effects read off from Theorem 4.1.
Proposition 4.3 (Advanced records are bridges). Once a bounding-box record lies outside the initial support, its first visit is white and its Tait edge has a fresh endpoint, hence is a bridge; so advanced record creation never raises \(\beta\), and unbounded cycle-rank production occurs inside explored territory.
Remark 4.4 (No monotone invariant). Active-loop length, signed area, \(E\), \(V\), \(k\), and \(\beta\) all fail to be monotone (e.g. the active loop drops from length \(20\) to \(4\) at step \(4\); the cycle rank takes every value in \(\{1,\dots,5\}\) during one mature period). No nonconstant affine combination of \((E,V,k,\beta)\) is universally monotone, ruling out the direct potential-function proofs.

5. Additive charges and clean translators

Definition 5.1. A clean finite translator with nonzero drift is a finite state with \(B_N=B_0+\drift\), \(p_N=p_0+\drift\), \(q_N=q_0\) for some \(N>0\) and \(\drift\neq0\): the entire black set translates, with no stationary residue and no wake.
Proposition 5.2. In \(\F_2[X^{\pm1},Y^{\pm1}]\) the charges \(A_t=\sum_{(x,y)\in B_t}X^x+[q_t\text{ vertical}]X^{x_t}\) and its row analogue \(D_t\) are conserved. A clean translator with \(\drift=(a,b)\) satisfies \(A=X^aA\), \(D=Y^bD\); since \(A(1)+D(1)=1\) in \(\F_2\), \(a\) and \(b\) are not both nonzero. Every clean translator is axis-aligned.
Proof. On a step from a vertical cell the toggle at column \(x_t\) cancels the disappearing vertical correction; from a horizontal cell the post-turn motion is vertical, so \(x_{t+1}=x_t\) and the toggle cancels the appearing correction. Translation covariance gives \(A=X^aA\); in the integral domain \(a\neq0\Rightarrow A=0\), \(b\neq0\Rightarrow D=0\), but \(A(1)+D(1)=2|B|+1=1\neq0\).
Proposition 5.3 (Additive-charge cocycle). For an abelian group \(A\) and \(w:\Z^2\to A\), heading corrections \(F_q\) (with the step relations on \(\turnR\): \(F_{q+1}(z+u_{q+1})-F_q(z)=-w(z)\), on \(\turnL\): \(F_{q-1}(z+u_{q-1})-F_q(z)=+w(z)\)) making \(\sum_{z\in B}w(z)+F_q(p)\) conserved exist only if \(w\) satisfies the four-corner condition \begin{equation}\label{eq:cocycle} w(x{+}1,y{+}1)+w(x{+}1,y)+w(x,y{+}1)+w(x,y)=0\ \text{in }A \end{equation} and \(4w=0\). Conversely, when \(A=\Z/4\Z\) (so \(4w=0\) is automatic) the four-corner condition suffices. Over \(\F_2\) the solutions are \(w(x,y)=\alpha(x)+\beta(y)\).
Proof. Necessity. Eliminating the \(F_q\) around a plaquette yields \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\). Combining the four relations at a single cell \(z\) gives both \(F_S(z)=F_N(z)+2w(z)\) and \(F_S(z)=F_N(z)-2w(z)\), hence \(4w(z)=0\); e.g. \(w=(-1)^{x+y}\) over \(A=\Z\) satisfies \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\) but has no such \(F_q\). Sufficiency for \(\Z/4\Z\). The step relations give the diagonal increments \(F_N(x{+}1,y{+}1)-F_N(x,y)=-w(x,y)+w(x{+}1,y)\) and \(F_N(x{+}1,y{-}1)-F_N(x,y)=w(x,y{-}1)-w(x{+}1,y{-}1)\), defining \(F_N\) on each parity sublattice by integration. Around an elementary diamond these four increments sum to the four-corner expression \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\), which vanishes, so the integration is path-independent and \(F_N\) is well defined; then \(F_S=F_N-2w\) and \(F_E,F_W\) by the step relations are consistent because \(4w=0\).

A mod-four refinement (using the quadratic solution \(Q(x,y)=x^2+2xy\) of \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\) over \(\Z/4\)) strengthens a clean translator’s drift to \((4a,0)\) or \((0,4a)\), with \(N\ge8|\drift|\); we use only axis alignment below (see [10]).

Let \(\growth=\#\turnR-\#\turnL\) over one period. Lemma 2.4 gives \(\growth\equiv0\pmod4\), and \(\growth\ge0\) since \(\growth\) counts the odd stabilised classes of Theorem 3.1.

Proposition 5.4 (Canonical endpoint decomposition). The one-period signed toggle pattern decomposes as \begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp} D=S+(T_\drift-1)A,\qquad S=\sum_{i=1}^{\growth}\delta_{c_i}, \end{equation} where \((T_\drift A)(z)=A(z-\drift)\), \(A\) is a finite moving widget, and \(c_1,\dots,c_\growth\) are the bases of the \(\growth\) growing strands (one per odd stabilised class). For the standard word \(\growth=12\) and \(|B_n|=11+12n\).
Proof. Partition the finite support of \(D\) into translation classes under \(z\mapsto z+\drift\). For a class with base \(c\), write \(f(n)=D(c+n\drift)\), a finitely supported \(f:\Z\to\Z\). Put \(s=\sum_n f(n)\) and \(h=f-s\,\delta_0\) (so \(\sum_n h=0\)), and set \(a(n)=\sum_{m>n}h(m)\). Then \(a(n-1)-a(n)=h(n)\), i.e. \((T-1)a=h\) with \((Ta)(n)=a(n-1)\), so \(f=s\,\delta_0+(T-1)a\); and \(a\) is finitely supported because \(\sum h=0\) makes it vanish far to the left as well as far to the right. Summing over classes gives \(\eqref{eq:decomp}\) with \(A\) the sum of the class antiderivatives. The per-class total \(\sum_n f\) is \(+1\) for an odd class and \(0\) for an even one (by the alternating-visit rule), so \(S\) is a sum of \(\growth\) unit masses, one per odd class, and \(\growth=\#\turnR-\#\turnL\). The standard-word figures follow from \(|B_0|=11\) and \(|B_{n+1}|-|B_n|=12\).
Corollary 5.5 (Separation). If \(\growth=0\) a finite stationary set can be deleted to leave a clean finite translator with drift \(\drift\).
Proof. Here \(S=0\), so \(D=(T_\drift-1)A\). Let \(b_n\) be the \(\{0,1\}\) indicator of \(B_n\) at the \(n\)-th period boundary. Each cell is toggled a net \(D(z)\in\{-1,0,1\}\) per period and the period-\(k\) pattern is \(T_{k\drift}D\), so \[ b_n=b_0+\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}T_{k\drift}(T_\drift-1)A=b_0+(T_{n\drift}-1)A=R+T_{n\drift}A,\quad R:=b_0-A. \] For large \(n\) the supports of \(R\) and \(T_{n\drift}A\) are disjoint; since \(b_n\) is \(\{0,1\}\)-valued, \(R\) and \(A\) are too, and \(B_n=R\sqcup(A+n\drift)\). As the trace is periodic with nonzero drift, each cell is visited finitely often, so at a late period boundary the ant never revisits \(R\); deleting \(R\) changes no future turn and leaves the black set \(A+n\drift\), a clean translator.

By Corollary 5.5, a \(\growth=0\) trace reduces to a clean translator, which §§6–7 prove impossible by two independent routes; hence \(\growth\neq0\).

6. The even-winding theorem

The one step sketched in earlier accounts—the local parity identity—is proved in full as Lemma 6.4.

Theorem 6.1 (Even-winding theorem). No finite-support clean translator with nonzero drift exists.

Fix a hypothetical clean translator. Its drift is axis-aligned (Proposition 5.2). A clean translator has \(\growth=0\), so its period \(N=2\#\turnR\) is even; since each step flips \(x+y\) parity, \(a+b\equiv N\equiv0\pmod2\), so the nonzero drift coordinate is even. After rotation/reflection write \(\drift=(L,0)\) with \(L>0\) even—all the argument needs. Reversibility gives \(A_{nN+j}=A_j+n\drift\), \(p_{nN+j}=p_j+n\drift\). Quotient the bi-infinite trace by \(\drift\) to a closed walk on the cylinder \(C_L=(\Z/L\Z)\times\Z\); \(L\) even makes the HV type descend. The multiplicity of an edge is its number of traversals by the projected one-period walk.

Lemma 6.2 (Visit count). Every vertex \(v\) of \(C_L\) is visited \(n_v=2k_v\) times in one quotient period, with exactly \(k_v\) turns \(\turnR\) and \(k_v\) turns \(\turnL\).
Proof. Let \(z\) represent \(v\). Each phase \(j\) with \(p_j\in v+\Z\drift\) has a unique level \(m_j\) with \(p_j=z+m_j\drift\), and phase \(j\) in period \(-m_j\) visits \(z\); conversely every visit to \(z\) arises once. So visits to \(v\) in one quotient period biject with visits to \(z\) over the translated bi-infinite orbit. Since the pattern is a finite translate at each phase, \(z\) is white far in both time directions; its turns alternate, begin with \(\turnR\), end with \(\turnL\), equally many of each.

At \(v\) let \(H_{v,\pm},V_{v,\pm}\) be the west/east and south/north edge multiplicities. One pair carries all arrivals, the other all departures, so \begin{equation}\label{eq:balance} H_{v,-}+H_{v,+}=V_{v,-}+V_{v,+}=2k_v . \end{equation}

Lemma 6.3 (Edge parities). The horizontal-edge multiplicity has a constant parity \(h_y\in\F_2\) along each row \(y\). Every vertical-edge multiplicity is even; write \(V_e=2G_e\).
Proof. Reducing \(\eqref{eq:balance}\) mod \(2\) gives \(H_{v,-}\equiv H_{v,+}\) (row-constant \(h_y\)) and \(V_{v,-}\equiv V_{v,+}\) (column-constant vertical parity). Finite vertical support forces a far-away vertical edge to multiplicity \(0\); constancy then makes every vertical multiplicity even.
Lemma 6.4 (Local parity). At every vertex \(v=(x,y)\), \(\;k_v\equiv h_y\pmod2\).
Proof. Suppose \(v\) is vertical (horizontal is the rotation). The ant arrives vertically and departs horizontally. Classify visits by (arrival direction, turn): from the south with \(\turnR\) departs east; south, \(\turnL\) west; north, \(\turnR\) west; north, \(\turnL\) east. Let \(a_{\turnR},a_{\turnL}\) count south arrivals and \(b_{\turnR},b_{\turnL}\) north arrivals by turn. The east edge is only ever traversed out of \(v\), so \(H_{v,+}=a_{\turnR}+b_{\turnL}\); the north edge only into \(v\), so \(V_{v,+}=b_{\turnR}+b_{\turnL}\); and \(k_v=a_{\turnR}+b_{\turnR}\). Then \[ H_{v,+}-k_v=b_{\turnL}-b_{\turnR}\equiv b_{\turnL}+b_{\turnR}=V_{v,+}\pmod2 . \] By Lemma 6.3, \(H_{v,+}\) has parity \(h_y\) and \(V_{v,+}\) is even, so \(h_y\equiv k_v\).
Proof of Theorem 6.1. Halving the vertical part of \(\eqref{eq:balance}\) with \(V_e=2G_e\) gives \(k_{x,y}=G_{x,y-1/2}+G_{x,y+1/2}\). Summing Lemma 6.4 over a column, \begin{equation}\label{eq:telescope} \sum_y h_y\equiv\sum_y k_{x,y}=\sum_y\bigl(G_{x,y-1/2}+G_{x,y+1/2}\bigr)\equiv0\pmod2, \end{equation} each interior half-edge counted twice and the exterior terms vanishing. Cut \(C_L\) along a vertical seam: the winding number \(w\) is the signed seam-crossing count, one horizontal seam edge per row of parity \(h_y\), so \(w\equiv\sum_y h_y\equiv0\pmod2\). But the lift runs from \(p\) to \(p+(L,0)\), so \(w=1\). Contradiction; reflection and rotation cover the remaining drifts.
Corollary 6.5 (Growth is a positive multiple of four). Every finite-support eventually-translating periodic trajectory has \(\growth\in4\Z_{>0}\); every highway builds a nonempty wake.
Proof. Zero drift is excluded by Theorem 2.3; negative growth cannot recur as \(b_t\ge0\); a heading-reset period has \(\growth\equiv0\pmod4\). A \(\growth=0\) nonzero-drift trace separates into a residue plus a clean translator, impossible by Theorem 6.1. Hence \(\growth\in4\Z_{>0}\).

The telescope \(\eqref{eq:telescope}\) is the finite algebraic core machine-checked in Lean (Appendix B). The standard highway has \(\growth=12\).

7. The signed mod-four wake-residue theorem

A second, independent proof that no zero-growth highway exists, which also pins the growth–drift arithmetic. Recall the strand bases \(c_1,\dots,c_\growth\).

Theorem 7.1 (Signed mod-four wake-residue identity). For a finite-support repeating trace with restored heading, nonzero drift \(\drift=(a,b)\), and decomposition \(\eqref{eq:decomp}\): (i) if \(a\neq0\), for every \(r\in\Z/|a|\Z\), \[ \sum_{i:\;c_{i,x}\equiv r\ (|a|)}\che(c_i)\equiv 2\pmod4; \] (ii) if \(b\neq0\), the analogous residue sum modulo \(|b|\) is \(\equiv2\pmod4\); (iii) if \(a=0\), the signed sum over each exact column is \(\equiv0\pmod4\) (symmetrically for rows if \(b=0\)).
Lemma 7.2 (Diagonal increment). Fix \(a\neq0\), \(\alpha:\Z/|a|\Z\to\Z/4\Z\), and \(w(x,y)=\che(x,y)\alpha(x)\), valued in \(\Z/4\Z\). Then \(w\) satisfies \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\), so potentials \(F_q\) exist, and \begin{equation}\label{eq:diag} F_N(x{+}1,y{+}1)-F_N(x,y)=-\che(x,y)\bigl(\alpha(x)+\alpha(x{+}1)\bigr), \end{equation} while \(F_N\) is \(2\)-periodic in \(y\).
Proof. Since \(\che(x{+}1,y{+}1)=\che(x,y)\) and \(\che(x{+}1,y)=\che(x,y{+}1)=-\che(x,y)\), the four-corner sum vanishes; so \(F_q\) exist by Proposition 5.3. Compose the \(\turnR\) step at \((x,y)\) heading \(N\) with the \(\turnL\) step at \((x{+}1,y)\) heading \(E\): adding \(F_E(x{+}1,y)-F_N(x,y)=-w(x,y)\) and \(F_N(x{+}1,y{+}1)-F_E(x{+}1,y)=w(x{+}1,y)\) gives \(\eqref{eq:diag}\). The relation \(F_S=F_N-2w\) gives the same increment along \((1,-1)\); combining over a closed square yields \(F_N(x,y{+}2)=F_N(x,y)\).
Proof of Theorem 7.1. Restored heading makes \(a+b\) even, so \(w(z+\drift)=w(z)\). Telescoping \(\eqref{eq:diag}\) across one horizontal residue cycle and using the \(2\)-periodicity gives the monodromy \(F_q(z+\drift)-F_q(z)\equiv 2\sum_r\alpha(r)\pmod4\) (the total is \(-2\che\sum_r\alpha\), and \(-2\equiv2\)). Charge conservation over one period, \(\sum_z D(z)w(z)+F_q(p+\drift)-F_q(p)=0\), with \(\drift\)-periodic \(w\) cancelling the widget terms, reduces via \(D=S+(T_\drift-1)A\) to \[ \sum_{i=1}^{\growth}\che(c_i)\alpha(c_{i,x})+2\sum_{r}\alpha(r)\equiv0\pmod4 . \] Taking \(\alpha=\mathbf 1_{\{r\}}\) gives (i); rotation gives (ii); a finitely supported exact-column \(\alpha\) with even \(b\) gives (iii).
Corollary 7.3 (Strand-density bound). Every finite-support repeating trace with drift \((a,b)\) has \(\growth\ge2\max(|a|,|b|)\); in particular \(\growth=0\) is impossible. A two-strand fiber has both bases of the same checkerboard parity; for \(\drift=(\pm2,\pm2)\), \(\growth=4\) the \(2\times2\) residue-count matrix is \(\left[\begin{smallmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{smallmatrix}\right]\) or its transpose.
Proof. A sum of \(m\) signs \(\pm1\) congruent to \(2\pmod4\) is nonzero with \(m\) positive and even; so each residue class mod \(|a|\) has \(\ge2\) bases, giving \(\growth\ge2|a|\), and symmetrically \(\growth\ge2|b|\). If \(\growth=0\) some class is empty while \(\drift\neq0\), a contradiction.
Remark (not sharp). The bound is a genuine lower bound but is not claimed attained by a chronological highway: the standard highway has \(\growth=12\) against \(2\max(2,2)=4\). Equality holds for the static aggregate edge-flow profiles satisfying the residue identity, but whether any is realised by a single ant tour is a chronology question left open in §10.

Unlike the even-winding argument, Theorem 7.1 uses neither axis alignment nor the clean-packet reduction. Independent computation supports it where testable (Appendix C): the underlying toggle-charge identity holds on all \(172{,}092\) heading-reset nonzero-drift words through length \(18\), while the strand identity and the bound \(\growth\ge2\max(|a|,|b|)\)—statements about realisable traces, of which none of period \(\le18\) exists—are confirmed on the standard highway, its powers, and its dihedral images.

8. Collision chains and the periodic touch graph

Encode a finite cell set as edges \((x,y)\mapsto C_xR_y\) of the bipartite row–column incidence graph; to a state \(s=(p,q)\) assign \(\eta(s)=C_{p_x}\) if \(q\) is vertical, \(R_{p_y}\) if horizontal.

Theorem 8.1 (Two-endpoint collision-chain parity). Over \(\F_2\), the boundary of the incidence graph of \(B_s\triangle B_t\) equals \(\eta(s)+\eta(t)\); intermediate tokens telescope, so at most one defect component is non-Eulerian and a same-pose return (\(\eta(s)=\eta(t)\)) has only Eulerian defect components.
Proof. One step toggles the single edge \(C_{p_x}R_{p_y}\), flipping the degree parity of exactly \(C_{p_x}\) and \(R_{p_y}\); the pre/post tokens differ by the incidence-vertex whose axis matches the heading, so a single step contributes \(\eta(s)+\eta(s')\) to the boundary. Summing telescopes to \(\eta(s)+\eta(t)\). A non-Eulerian component needs nonempty boundary, so at most one exists, absent when \(\eta(s)=\eta(t)\).

This gives a four-cell lower bound. Take \(s\) just after an \(\turnR\) visit to \(z=(x,y)\) and \(t\) just before its next \(\turnL\) visit. Then \(z\) is black at both times, so its edge \(C_xR_y\) is absent from \(G:=B_s\triangle B_t\); and since one state is horizontal at \(z\) and the other vertical, \(\eta(s)+\eta(t)=C_x+R_y\). By Theorem 8.1, \(\partial G=C_x+R_y\), so \(C_x\) and \(R_y\) are joined in \(G\) by a path avoiding \(C_xR_y\); in the simple bipartite incidence graph that path has at least three edges, and adjoining \(C_xR_y\) closes a cycle of length at least four. Hence the completed \(\turnR\)-to-\(\turnL\) lifetime involves at least four distinct cells.

We close with an observation, stated as such rather than a theorem because a self-contained development of the touch-graph formalism is lengthy; that development, with full definitions and proofs, is deferred to the companion report, and we use here only its numerical output.

Observation 8.2 (Periodic touch-graph rank). Pair every stabilised \(\turnR\) with its following \(\turnL\) into a \(4\)-valent temporal vertex; the wake \(\turnR\) events become degree-two \(H/V\) switches, whose suppression yields a connected \(4\)-regular Euler system, to which Traldi’s circuit-partition/interlacement theory [9],[8] applies. With \(m\) paired lifetimes, \(c\) closed \(H/V\) smoothing circuits, and \(\kappa\) cycles in the wake-edge subgraph \(W\), \(\dim Z(T)=m-c+1\), \(\dim Z(T/W)=m-c-\kappa+1\), and \(0\to Z(W)\to Z(T)\to Z(T/W)\to0\); the lifetime rows span \(Z(T/W)\) with codimension \(\le\kappa\) in \(Z(T)\). On the standard trace one computes \(m=46,c=5,\kappa=1\), projected rank \(41\), boundary rank \(42\).

Ordinary circuit-nullity closes the contracted graph but does not by itself supply the \(\kappa\)-dimensional wake-cycle restriction; at fixed \(\growth=4\) an explicit abstract family has \(c\to\infty\), so no bound depending only on \(\growth\) follows from the graph theory alone.

9. Small-period exclusion

All searches are exact (integer coordinates, exact finite sets). Two independent engines are used: a reference implementation of Theorem 3.1, and a residue-pruned engine using Corollary 6.5 and Theorem 7.1. Both accept the standard word.

Theorem 9.1 (No periodic highway of period at most \(48\)). No finite-support periodic highway of nonzero drift has period at most \(48\). Consequently any nonstandard finite-support periodic highway has even period at least \(50\) (periods \(50,52,\dots,102\) are not addressed here).
Verification. Odd periods restore no heading, and Corollary 6.5 excludes zero growth, so we search even periods for positive growth: (a) complete enumeration through period \(32\) (\(46{,}185{,}421\) nodes); (b) positive-growth searches at \(34,36,38,40\) (up to \(2.0\times10^{8}\) nodes; zero highways); (c) two-engine rank-audited searches at \(42,44,46,48\). At period \(48\) both engines visited the identical \(8{,}677{,}026{,}370\) nodes and \(451{,}962{,}870\) leaves over all \(2^{16}\) prefix ranks with no node cap; the residue engine rejected \(439{,}705{,}662\) leaves and applied the exact criterion to \(12{,}257{,}208\); both returned zero certificates with all per-rank counters in agreement.

10. The remaining gap

Problem 10.1 (Bounded retreat / bounded active core). Show that for every finite-support configuration there is a time after which the ant stays within a bounded corridor behind its record frontier. A stronger Tait-graph formulation that would also suffice is that the active boundary component at or ahead of the record has uniformly bounded size and shed components are never reabsorbed.

Under either, the frontier window has finitely many states modulo translation, determinism forces periodicity, and Theorem 3.1 classifies the cycle. The easy routes fail: unboundedness permits an ever-enlarging two-dimensional region; no scalar Tait invariant is monotone (Remark 4.4); and local pumping is invalid because equal local cores may connect through distant territory, turning a merge into a split (Theorem 4.1).

Problem 10.2 (Periodic classification). Show that every positive-growth periodic trace admitted by finite support is the standard period-\(104\) trace up to symmetry and phase.

At \(\growth=4\) the residue skeletons (Theorem 7.1) and touch-graph rank (Observation 8.2) leave unbounded free parameters, and explicit heading-reset countermodels satisfy every incidence/residue condition yet fail the cross-period criterion. A resolution needs a single-tour chronology theorem for unbounded period. A disproof would exhibit an infinite certificate; none has been found.

11. Concluding remarks

We have developed a structural and arithmetic theory of the classical finite-support periodic highway: a decidable realisability criterion, an exact medial-graph conjugacy, two independent proofs that no zero-growth clean translator exists (so every highway grows a wake of size \(\in4\Z_{>0}\)), the lower bound \(\growth\ge2\|\drift\|_\infty\), a collision-chain parity theorem and a touch-graph rank formula, and an exhaustive exclusion of all periodic highways of period at most \(48\). The algebraic cores are machine-checked in Lean 4. The outstanding difficulty is a bounded-retreat / bounded-active-core lemma together with a single-tour chronology theorem—these, not the arithmetic constraints proved here, are what a full solution must still supply.

Appendix A. Worked example: the standard highway

Normalised so the ant faces north at the origin and the trace begins with \(\turnR\) at a global minimum of the black-count walk, the period-\(104\) word is

RRRRLLRLLRRRRLLRRRRLLRLRRRRLRLLLLRRRRLRRLRRRRLLLLRLRRRRLRRRRLLLLRLRRRRLRLLRRLLLLRRLLRRRRLLRRLRLLRLLRLRLL

with \(\#\turnR-\#\turnL=12\) and drift \((2,-2)\), consistent with Corollary 6.5 (\(12\in4\Z_{>0}\)) and Corollary 7.3 (\(12\ge2\max(2,2)\)). Applying the seed construction of Theorem 3.1 to this exact word (in this exact phase and orientation) gives the \(11\)-cell black seed \[ \begin{aligned}&(-2,-2),(-2,-1),(-1,0),(0,1),(1,-2),(1,1),\\&(2,-2),(2,0),(3,-1),(3,0),(4,0),\end{aligned} \] which with a north-facing ant at the origin produces the exact word above immediately, restoring the north heading and translating by \((2,-2)\). (Both the word and this seed were verified by direct simulation. The phase obtained by cyclically shifting this word by \(100\) symbols begins with \(\turnL\) and has drift \((-2,2)\), and is generated by a different, \(13\)-cell seed; the two phases must not be interchanged.)

Appendix B. Lean 4 formalization

A dependency-light Lean 4 project (Lean \(4.32.0\), standard library) checks the exact transition kernel and the algebraic cores above. Every audited theorem depends only on \(\texttt{propext}\), \(\texttt{Classical.choice}\), \(\texttt{Quot.sound}\); there are no \(\texttt{sorry}\)s, no custom axioms, no \(\texttt{native\_decide}\). The finite parity telescope \(\eqref{eq:telescope}\)—the heart of the even-winding theorem—is checked as evenWindingParityCore:

def bxor : Bool -> Bool -> Bool
  | false, b     => b
  | true,  false => true
  | true,  true  => false

def xorAll : List Bool -> Bool
  | []      => false
  | b :: bs => bxor b (xorAll bs)

def incidences : List Bool -> List Bool
  | []             => []
  | [_]            => []
  | a :: b :: rest => bxor a b :: incidences (b :: rest)

-- XOR of all adjacent incidences of a list bracketed by two `false`
-- endpoints is `false`: the seam-crossing count is even.
theorem evenWindingParityCore (interior : List Bool) :
    xorAll (incidences (false :: interior ++ [false])) = false := by
  rw [xorAll_incidences, lastFrom_append_singleton]; rfl

The formalization isolates, as an explicit interface, the endpoint normal form and lifted potential geometry of a genuine translator, so Theorem 7.1 is certified conditional on a structured trace certificate rather than end to end. The project and build script accompany the paper [10].

Appendix C. Computational methodology

The search space is the tree of legal prefixes. By Proposition 2.2 it suffices to search words beginning with \(\turnR\), and by Corollary 6.5 only positive-growth heading-reset words. A period-\(N\) search partitions the length-\(k\) prefix-rank interval \([0,2^{k-1})\) into disjoint worker intervals; each worker enumerates its subtree depth-first and applies Theorem 3.1 at every leaf. A complete exclusion requires every rank covered once, every shard complete with no node cap, empty hit lists, and aggregate counters equal to the per-rank sums. Two independent engines are run for periods \(42\)–\(48\); agreement of all per-rank counters is the cross-audit. The residue identity was validated by enumerating all \(524{,}286\) words through length \(18\). Sources, per-shard records with SHA-256 manifests, and exact commands accompany the paper [10].
