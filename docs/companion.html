Structural and Computational Results on the Finite-Support Highway Conjecture for Langton’s Ant

Atharva Jillhewar
Preprint — 2026
Status. This paper collects rigorous structural and computational results on the finite-support highway conjecture for Langton’s ant. It does not claim a proof or disproof of the conjecture, which remains open; the precise missing ingredient is identified in §11.
Abstract. Langton’s ant is a deterministic reversible cellular automaton on \(\Z^2\) whose long-term behaviour, for every finite initial colouring, is empirically the formation of a period-\(104\) “highway”. Whether this holds for every finite initial black support is a well-known open problem. This paper collects, in a uniform and rigorous framework, a body of structural and computational results on this conjecture. We prove: an exact, decidable necessary-and-sufficient criterion for an infinite periodic turn word to be the trace of some finite-support colouring (Theorem 3.1); a heading/black-count mod-four invariant and a reduction of the two-dimensional orbit to a one-dimensional black-count walk; an exact conjugacy of the ant with edge-toggling on a Tait (medial) graph, together with a cycle-rank surgery identity; and two independent proofs that no finite-support clean translator with zero net growth exists—one homological (the even-winding theorem) and one via a signed mod-four wake-residue identity, which further yields the strand-density bound \(\growth\ge 2\|\drift\|_\infty\) for every finite-support repeating trace. Consequently every finite-support periodic highway has net black growth a positive multiple of four. On the computational side we give an exact finite black seed for the standard highway, certify all \(512\) centered \(3\times3\) patterns, and exclude by two independently implemented, rank-audited exhaustive searches every finite-support periodic highway of nonzero drift and period at most \(48\). We delimit precisely the single geometric gap—a bounded-retreat / bounded active core lemma—that separates these results from a full solution, record the partial exterior-dynamics and interlacement reductions aimed at it, and describe a Lean 4 formalization of the algebraic core. No claim of a proof or disproof of the highway conjecture is made.

1. Introduction

1.1. The automaton and the conjecture

Langton’s ant moves on the square lattice \(\Z^2\), each cell coloured white (\(0\)) or black (\(1\)). A pointer (the ant) occupies a cell and faces one of the four cardinal directions. One step of the dynamics is: if the current cell is white, turn right (\(90^\circ\) clockwise); if it is black, turn left; then flip the colour of the current cell and move one unit forward in the new heading. We always assume the finite-support condition: the set of initially black cells is finite. Translating and rotating, we normalise the initial ant to be at the origin facing north.

Iterating from the all-white configuration produces, after a transient of \(9977\) steps, a self-similar diagonal structure—the highway—that repeats with least period \(104\) and displaces the ant by \((\pm2,\pm2)\) per period. Extensive experiment suggests the same eventual behaviour for every finite initial colouring.

Conjecture 1.1 (Finite-support highway conjecture). For every finite initial black set \(B_0\subset\Z^2\) and every initial pose, the orbit of Langton’s ant eventually enters a translate, rotation, reflection and phase shift of the standard period-\(104\) highway.

Conjecture 1.1 is stronger than unboundedness of the trajectory (that the ant visits infinitely many cells), which is a theorem of Bunimovich and Troubetzkoy [1]. It is also resistant to purely finite computation: finite patterns can force the ant to simulate arbitrary Boolean circuits and, on periodic backgrounds, Turing machines [2], so no empirical time cutoff can decide the conjecture. The symbolic dynamics of the ant has genuine infinite-memory effects [3]. Recent work classifies possible highways and studies confinement times [4],[5],[6], but Conjecture 1.1 remains open.

1.2. What is proved here, and what is not

This paper is deliberately explicit about status. We prove a number of exact structural theorems and finite computational theorems; none of them resolves Conjecture 1.1, and we identify precisely the missing geometric ingredient (§11). Every statement below is labelled as an exact theorem, a finite computational theorem, a conjecture, or an explicitly open problem. The main contributions are:

  1. An exact periodic-trace criterion (Theorem 3.1) turning “is \(w^\omega\) a finite-support highway?” into a finite computation, with an explicit seed.
  2. The heading/black-count invariant and the black-count path reduction (§2.3).
  3. The Tait-graph / boundary-loop conjugacy and the cycle-rank surgery identity (§4).
  4. The even-winding theorem: no zero-growth clean translator (Theorem 6.1), hence growth \(\in 4\Z_{>0}\) for every highway.
  5. The signed mod-four wake-residue theorem (Theorem 7.1) and the strand-density bound \(\growth\ge 2\max(|a|,|b|)\).
  6. Two-engine, rank-audited exclusion of all finite-support periodic highways of period \(\le 48\) (§10).
  7. The exterior-dynamics, collision-chain, and interlacement reductions of the remaining gap (§§8–9).
  8. A Lean 4 formalization of the algebraic core (Appendix A).

The presentation records failed and merely heuristic arguments honestly (§11), because they delimit the true difficulty of the problem.

2. Conventions and elementary invariants

2.1. State, step, trace

A state is a triple \(s=(B,p,q)\) with \(B\subset\Z^2\) finite (the black set), \(p\in\Z^2\) (the ant position) and \(q\in\Z/4\Z\) the heading, numbered \(0=N,1=E,2=S,3=W\). Let \(u_0=(0,1),u_1=(1,0),u_2=(0,-1),u_3=(-1,0)\) be the unit heading vectors. The turn at \(s\) is \(\turnR\) (right) if \(p\notin B\) and \(\turnL\) (left) if \(p\in B\); write \(\varepsilon=+1\) for \(\turnR\) and \(\varepsilon=-1\) for \(\turnL\). One step is \[ s\mapsto\bigl(B\,\triangle\,\{p\},\; p+u_{q+\varepsilon},\; q+\varepsilon\bigr), \] where \(\triangle\) is symmetric difference. The trace is the infinite word of turns encountered. The dynamics is reversible, so orbits never merge.

2.2. HV partition and alternating visits

Proposition 2.1 (P1: HV/checkerboard partition). Along any orbit the heading parity \(q_t\bmod 2\) and the checkerboard parity \((p_{t,x}+p_{t,y})\bmod 2\) each change at every step, so their difference is a constant of the motion. Consequently each cell \(z\) is entered by the ant always horizontally or always vertically; the admissible directed lattice edges have a fixed orientation, and each cell has exactly two incoming and two outgoing directed edges.
Proof. Each step turns by \(\pm1\) quarter turn, so \(q_{t+1}-q_t\) is odd and \(q_t\bmod2\) alternates. The displacement \(u_{q_{t+1}}\) has odd \(L^1\)-length, so \((p_{t,x}+p_{t,y})\bmod 2\) also alternates; their sum is invariant. Fixing a cell \(z\), whenever \(p_t=z\) the checkerboard parity is fixed, hence so is \(q_t\bmod2\): the arrival is always horizontal or always vertical.

Call a cell V-type if the ant always arrives vertically and H-type otherwise. We write \(\che(x,y)=(-1)^{x+y}\) for the checkerboard sign.

Proposition 2.2 (P2: alternating visit rule). At any fixed cell the turns encountered alternate strictly. Since a cell is white iff it will next cause an \(\turnR\), and only finitely many cells are initially black, all but finitely many cells have \(\turnR\) as their first encountered turn.
Theorem 2.3 (Unboundedness [1]). Every finite-support orbit visits infinitely many cells.
Proof sketch. If the visited set were finite, then by reversibility and finiteness the orbit would be periodic. Take a visited cell \(z\) maximising \(x+y\); its north and east neighbours are unvisited. By Proposition 2.1, if \(z\) is entered horizontally the ant enters from the west, and the two successive visits leave south then north—forcing a visit to an unvisited maximal-corner neighbour, a contradiction. The vertical case is rotated.

2.3. The heading/black-count invariant and path reduction

Let \(B_t,p_t,q_t\) be the state before step \(t\), \(b_t=|B_t|\), and \(r_t=b_{t+1}-b_t\in\{\pm1\}\).

Proposition 2.4 (P4, P9: invariant and path reduction). For all \(t\), \begin{equation}\label{eq:P4} q_t-b_t\equiv q_0-b_0\pmod 4, \end{equation} and the two-dimensional path is a function of the one-dimensional black-count walk: \begin{equation}\label{eq:P9} p_{t+1}-p_t=u_{\,q_0-b_0+b_{t+1}}. \end{equation} In particular a heading-resetting period changes \(b\) by a multiple of \(4\).
Proof. A white/\(\turnR\) step has \(r_t=+1\) and \(q_{t+1}-q_t=+1\); a black/\(\turnL\) step has both differences \(-1\). Thus \(q_t-b_t\) is invariant, giving \(\eqref{eq:P4}\). The move occurs after the turn, so \(q_{t+1}=q_0-b_0+b_{t+1}\pmod4\) and \(\eqref{eq:P9}\) follows.

Equation \(\eqref{eq:P9}\) does not say every nonnegative nearest-neighbour walk is colour-realisable: repeated visits must still request alternating turns. Differentiating the centred first moment \(J_t=\sum_{z\in B_t}(z-p_t)\) gives the exact identity \begin{equation}\label{eq:P10} J_{t+1}-J_t=-\,b_{t+1}\,u_{\,q_0-b_0+b_{t+1}}, \end{equation} invariant under simultaneous translation of the black set and the ant (P10); it quantifies the wake of a growing highway.

3. The exact periodic-trace criterion

Fix an initial heading \(q_0\) and a proposed finite turn word \(w=w_0\cdots w_{N-1}\). Compute the path \(p_0,\dots,p_N\) from \(w\) (Proposition 2.1 makes each step well defined). Suppose \(q_N=q_0\) and the displacement \(\drift=p_N-p_0\neq 0\). Define \(i\sim j\iff p_i-p_j\in\Z\drift\); for each class \(I\) pick \(b_I\) and integers \(a_i\) with \(p_i=b_I+a_i\drift\). For \(n\ge\min_I a_i\), let \(S_{I,n}\) be the word of all \(w_i\) (\(i\in I\)) with \(a_i\le n\), ordered by \((n-a_i,\,i)\).

Theorem 3.1 (P3: finite-support periodic-trace criterion). The infinite word \(w^\omega\) is the trace of Langton’s ant from some finite-support colouring if and only if, for every class \(I\): (i) every \(S_{I,n}\) with \(\min_I a_i\le n\le\max_I a_i\) alternates strictly between \(\turnR\) and \(\turnL\); and (ii) the stabilised word \(S_{I,\max_I a_i}\) begins with \(\turnR\). When these hold, an explicit finite black seed is \[ B=\bigl\{\,b_I+n\drift:\ n\lt \textstyle\max_I a_i\ \text{and}\ S_{I,n}\ \text{begins with}\ \turnL\,\bigr\}. \]
Proof. \(S_{I,n}\) is precisely the chronological turn sequence at the physical cell \(b_I+n\drift\) over the infinite trajectory \(w^\omega\): the occurrence of phase \(i\in I\) in period \(k\ge0\) is at \(p_i+k\drift=b_I+(a_i+k)\drift\), equal to \(b_I+n\drift\) exactly when \(k=n-a_i\ge0\), and chronological order is the lexicographic order of \((n-a_i,i)\).
Necessity. Each visit flips the cell, so its turn sequence alternates: (i) is necessary. Once \(n\ge\max_I a_i\) the sequence has stabilised, and infinitely many cells share this first turn; by Proposition 2.2 only finitely many cells may start with \(\turnL\), so the stable first turn is \(\turnR\): (ii) is necessary.
Sufficiency. Colour exactly \(B\) black. At each boundary cell the chosen colour reproduces the first symbol of \(S_{I,n}\), and strict alternation supplies every later symbol. At every stabilised cell (ii) makes the first encounter \(\turnR\), matching its white initial colour. Induction on chronological time shows the ant follows \(w^\omega\) forever; the position is displaced by \(\drift\neq0\) every \(N\) steps.
Remark 3.2. There is an equivalent one-sequence test: sort each class by decreasing \(a_i\), ties by increasing \(i\); every \(S_{I,n}\) is a suffix of this stabilised word, so it suffices to check that the single stabilised word alternates and begins with \(\turnR\). This is the checker used in all computations of §10.

4. The Tait-graph / boundary-loop conjugacy

Normalise the HV partition so that even cells receive vertical arrivals. In the frozen all-white routing the admissible directed arcs form clockwise directed four-cycles around one checkerboard class of unit plaquettes. Take those plaquettes, labelled by their lower-left corners, as vertices of a rotated square lattice. Each ant cell \(z=(x,y)\) corresponds to the Tait edge \begin{equation}\label{eq:tait} e_z=\begin{cases}\{(x,y-1),(x-1,y)\}, & x+y \text{ even},\\ \{(x-1,y-1),(x,y)\}, & x+y \text{ odd}.\end{cases} \end{equation} Let \(H_B=\{e_z:z\in B\}\) be the finite edge subgraph of black cells.

Theorem 4.1 (P5: boundary-loop conjugacy). White and black are the two smoothings of the corresponding directed crossing. Consequently: (a) the frozen routing loops are exactly the boundary components of a regular neighbourhood of \(H_B\), together with the untouched four-cycles; (b) the ant traverses its current boundary loop and then toggles \(e_z\); and (c) every step either merges two frozen loops into one or splits one loop into two. This is an exact conjugacy.

Let \(E=|B|\), \(V\) the number of incident Tait vertices, \(k\) the number of nonempty components, and \(\beta=E-V+k\) the cycle rank.

Theorem 4.2 (P6: cycle-rank surgery identity). A regular neighbourhood of \(H_B\) has \(k+\beta=E-V+2k\) boundary components. Relative to the \(V\) separate all-white plaquette loops, the change in loop count is \begin{equation}\label{eq:cyclerank} \Delta C=E-2V+2k,\qquad E+\Delta C=2\beta . \end{equation} Adding a bridge merges two loops and preserves \(\beta\); adding a cycle edge splits a loop and raises \(\beta\) by one. Removing a bridge splits a loop and preserves \(\beta\); removing a nonbridge merges two loops and lowers \(\beta\) by one.
Proof. A regular neighbourhood of a graph with \(V\) vertices, \(E\) edges and \(k\) components is a surface of Euler characteristic \(V-E\) with \(k\) pieces; its boundary has \(k+\beta\) circles where \(\beta=E-V+k\). Formula \(\eqref{eq:cyclerank}\) is the difference from the \(V\) trivial loops, and the surgery cases are the standard bridge/cycle-edge effects read off from Theorem 4.1(c).
Proposition 4.3 (P7: eventual record edges are bridges). Once a bounding-box record lies outside the finite initial support, its first visit is white, and a new east/north/west/south record turns south/east/north/west. Its Tait edge has a previously untouched endpoint, so it is a bridge. Hence sufficiently advanced record creation cannot increase \(\beta\): any unbounded production of cycle rank occurs strictly inside already-explored territory.

The standard highway is rigid here: after a short settling phase its active component is a translated unicyclic graph with \(16\) vertices and \(16\) edges; each \(104\)-step period sheds a separate \(13\)-vertex, \(12\)-edge tree while reproducing the active core translated by \((-2,2)\), and total cycle rank remains \(2\).

Remark 4.4 (No monotone invariant). Active-loop length, its signed area/orientation, \(E,V,k\) and \(\beta\) all fail to be monotone, with explicit failures on the blank orbit: at step \(4\) the active loop drops from length \(20\) to \(4\); at time \(14\) it is a counterclockwise four-cycle; during one mature period the cycle rank takes every value in \(\{1,\dots,5\}\). Exact compact states realise both signs of every independent local change vector in \((E,V,k,\beta)\), so no nonconstant affine combination of these four quantities is universally monotone.

5. Charges and clean-translator restrictions

Definition 5.1. A clean finite translator with nonzero drift is a finite state such that for some \(N>0\) and \(\drift\in\Z^2\setminus\{0\}\), \(B_N=B_0+\drift\), \(p_N=p_0+\drift\), \(q_N=q_0\). “Clean” means the complete black set translates: no stationary debris, no newly emitted wake. This is not the ordinary highway, which grows a wake.

5.1. Additive \(\F_2\) charges and axis alignment

Work in the Laurent ring \(\F_2[X^{\pm1},Y^{\pm1}]\). Let \(V_t=1\) if \(q_t\) is vertical, \(H_t=1-V_t\), and \[ A_t=\sum_{(x,y)\in B_t}X^x+V_tX^{x_t},\qquad D_t=\sum_{(x,y)\in B_t}Y^y+H_tY^{y_t}. \]

Proposition 5.2 (P11, Step 4). \(A_t\) and \(D_t\) are conserved exactly. Hence a clean translator with \(\drift=(a,b)\) satisfies \(A=X^aA\) and \(D=Y^bD\) in the integral domain \(\F_2[X^{\pm1},Y^{\pm1}]\); since \(A(1)=|B|+V\) and \(D(1)=|B|+H\) cannot both vanish (\(V+H=1\)), \(a\) and \(b\) cannot both be nonzero. Every clean translator is axis-aligned.
Proof. For \(A_t\): if the incoming heading is vertical the cell toggle at column \(x_t\) cancels the disappearing vertical correction; if horizontal, the post-turn motion is vertical so \(x_{t+1}=x_t\) and the toggle cancels the newly appearing correction. Conservation and translation covariance give \(A=X^aA\); in an integral domain \(a\neq0\) forces \(A=0\), similarly \(b\neq0\) forces \(D=0\); but \(A(1)+D(1)=2|B|+1=1\neq0\) in \(\F_2\).
Proposition 5.3 (additive-charge completeness). An \(\F_2\) (or \(\Z/m\)) invariant additive over black cells with an ant-state correction \(f_q(z)\) and black-cell weight \(w(z)\) exists iff \(w\) solves the zero mixed-difference equation \begin{equation}\label{eq:cocycle} w(x{+}1,y{+}1)+w(x{+}1,y)+w(x,y{+}1)+w(x,y)=0 \pmod m,\qquad m\,w=0, \end{equation} whose solutions over \(\F_2\) are \(w(x,y)=\alpha(x)+\beta(y)\). Over \(\Z/4\) a further quadratic class \(Q(x,y)=x^2+2xy\) solves \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\) but yields no endpoint restriction beyond axis drift divisible by four.
Proposition 5.4 (P11, Step 5). A clean translator has period divisible by eight, drift \((4a,0)\) or \((0,4a)\), and \(N\ge 16\); the corner-free footprint bound gives \(N\ge8|\drift|\).

5.2. The strand decomposition

Let \(\growth=\#\turnR-\#\turnL\) be the net growth of one period. Restoring the heading forces \(\growth\equiv0\pmod4\), and \(\growth\ge0\) because \(\growth\) equals the number of odd stabilised translation classes.

Theorem 5.5 (P12: canonical endpoint decomposition). Every repeated trace has a canonical decomposition of its one-period signed toggle pattern \begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp} D=S+(T_\drift-1)A,\qquad S=\sum_{i=1}^{\growth}\delta_{c_i}, \end{equation} where \((T_\drift A)(z)=A(z-\drift)\), \(A\) is a finite moving widget, and the \(c_i\) are the bases of the \(\growth\) growing strands (one \(+1\) base per odd stabilised class). For the normalised standard word this is two stationary black cells, an \(11\)-cell moving widget, and \(12\) strands; hence \(|B_n|=13+12n\) at period boundaries.

If \(\growth=0\) then \(S=0\); the stationary part eventually separates and deleting it leaves a genuine clean translator. §§6–7 prove this is impossible.

6. The even-winding theorem

Theorem 6.1 (P15: no zero-growth clean translator). There is no finite black pattern and ant state that evolves in finitely many steps to exactly one nonzero translate of itself with restored heading and zero net black growth. Equivalently, no clean finite translator with nonzero drift exists.
Proof. Suppose \((A,p,q)\mapsto(A+\drift,p+\drift,q)\) in one word of length \(N\) with \(A\) finite and \(\drift\neq0\). By Propositions 5.2 and 5.4 the drift is axis-aligned with coordinate divisible by four; write \(\drift=(L,0)\), \(L\in4\Z_{>0}\).
Quotient and visit count. Reversibility and translation covariance give \(A_{nN+j}=A_j+n\drift\) and \(p_{nN+j}=p_j+n\drift\). Quotient the bi-infinite trace by \(\drift\) to a closed walk on the cylinder \(C_L=(\Z/L\Z)\times\Z\); the HV type descends because \(L\) is even. At a quotient vertex \(v\) with representative \(z\), the visits to \(v\) in one quotient period are exactly the visits to \(z\) over the translated bi-infinite orbit. Since the pattern is a finite translate at each phase, \(z\) is white far in both time directions; its turns alternate, begin with \(\turnR\), end with \(\turnL\), equally many of each: \begin{equation}\label{eq:visit} n_v=2k_v,\qquad k_v\ \turnR\text{'s and }k_v\ \turnL\text{'s}, \end{equation} with \(k_v=0\) allowed.
Edge multiplicities. By Proposition 2.1 each vertex has one pair of edges carrying all arrivals and the other all departures: \begin{equation}\label{eq:balance} H_{v,-}+H_{v,+}=V_{v,-}+V_{v,+}=2k_v. \end{equation} Reducing the horizontal equality mod \(2\) makes horizontal parity constant along each row, \(h_y\in\F_2\); the same up each column plus finite vertical support forces every vertical multiplicity even: \(V_e=2G_e\).
Local parity and telescope. A local HV parity computation gives \(k_v\equiv h_y\pmod2\) at both cell types; halving the vertical balance gives \(k_{x,y}=G_{x,y-1/2}+G_{x,y+1/2}\). Summing over a column, \begin{equation}\label{eq:telescope} \sum_y h_y\equiv\sum_y\bigl(G_{x,y-1/2}+G_{x,y+1/2}\bigr)\equiv0\pmod2, \end{equation} each interior half-edge appearing twice and the exterior terms vanishing.
Homological contradiction. Cut along a vertical seam: the winding number \(w\) is the signed seam-crossing count, one horizontal seam edge per row of parity \(h_y\), so \(w\equiv\sum_y h_y\equiv0\pmod2\). But the lift runs from \(p\) to \(p+(L,0)\) and winds exactly once, \(w=1\). Contradiction; reflection and rotation cover the remaining drifts.

The telescope \(\eqref{eq:telescope}\) is exactly the finite algebraic core machine-checked in Lean (Appendix A, evenWindingParityCore).

Corollary 6.2 (growth is a positive multiple of four). Every finite-support eventually-translating periodic trajectory has net black growth \begin{equation}\label{eq:growth4} \growth\in 4\Z_{>0}. \end{equation}
Proof. Zero drift confines the ant to finitely many positions, excluded by Theorem 2.3. Negative growth cannot repeat forever since \(b_t\ge0\). A heading-resetting period has \(\growth\equiv0\pmod4\). By Theorem 5.5 a \(\growth=0\) repeating trace with nonzero drift separates into stationary debris and a clean moving packet, contradicting Theorem 6.1. Hence \(\growth>0\) and \(\growth\in4\Z_{>0}\).

The ordinary highway has \(\growth=12\), consistent with \(\eqref{eq:growth4}\).

7. The signed mod-four wake-residue theorem

Recall \(\che(x,y)=(-1)^{x+y}\) and the strand bases \(c_1,\dots,c_\growth\) of Theorem 5.5.

Theorem 7.1 (P22: signed mod-four wake-residue identity). Let a finite-support repeating trace have restored heading, nonzero drift \(\drift=(a,b)\), growth \(\growth\), and canonical decomposition \(\eqref{eq:decomp}\). Then: (i) if \(a\neq0\), for every residue \(r\in\Z/|a|\Z\), \begin{equation}\label{eq:res-a} \sum_{i:\,c_{i,x}\equiv r\ (|a|)}\che(c_i)\equiv 2\pmod4; \end{equation} (ii) if \(b\neq0\), the analogous exact-row-residue sum is \(\equiv2\pmod4\); (iii) a zero drift coordinate gives signed sum \(\equiv0\pmod4\) over every exact column/row.
Proof. Fix \(a\neq0\) and any \(\alpha\colon\Z/|a|\Z\to\Z/4\Z\); set \begin{equation}\label{eq:weight} w(x,y)=\che(x,y)\,\alpha(x)\in\Z/4\Z . \end{equation} Then \(4w=0\) and \(w\) satisfies \(\eqref{eq:cocycle}\) since \(\che\) changes sign on each unit step; by Proposition 5.3 there are heading potentials \(F_q\) with, on an \(\turnR\) step \(F_{q+1}(z+u_{q+1})-F_q(z)=-w(z)\), and on an \(\turnL\) step \(F_{q-1}(z+u_{q-1})-F_q(z)=+w(z)\). Composing the north-to-east \(\turnR\) equation with the east-to-north \(\turnL\) equation gives \(F_N(z+(1,1))-F_N(z)=-\che(z)(\alpha(x)+\alpha(x+1))\), and via \(F_S=F_N-2w\) the same increment along \((1,-1)\); \(F_N\) is vertically two-periodic. Restored heading gives \(\growth\equiv0\pmod4\), so the period is even and \(a+b\) is even; hence \(\che\) and \(w\) are \(\drift\)-periodic. Telescoping one horizontal residue cycle yields the monodromy \begin{equation}\label{eq:monodromy} F_q(z+\drift)-F_q(z)\equiv 2\sum_{r\in\Z/|a|\Z}\alpha(r)\pmod4 \end{equation} for all four headings and either sign of \(a\) (the total is \(-2\che(z)\sum_r\alpha(r)\), and \(-2\equiv2\)). Charge conservation over one period, \begin{equation}\label{eq:conservation} \sum_z D(z)\,w(z)+F_q(p+\drift)-F_q(p)=0, \end{equation} with \(w\) being \(\drift\)-periodic and \((T_\drift A)(z)=A(z-\drift)\), cancels the widget terms; substituting \(D=S+(T_\drift-1)A\) and \(\eqref{eq:monodromy}\) gives \begin{equation}\label{eq:reduced} \sum_{i=1}^{\growth}\che(c_i)\,\alpha(c_{i,x})+2\sum_{r}\alpha(r)\equiv0\pmod4 . \end{equation} Choosing \(\alpha=\mathbf 1_{\{r\}}\) turns \(\eqref{eq:reduced}\) into \(\eqref{eq:res-a}\), using \(-2\equiv2\). Rotation gives (ii). For \(a=0\): a finitely supported exact-column \(\alpha\) with even \(b\) makes the monodromy zero and exact-column indicators give (iii).
Corollary 7.2 (P16: strand-density bound). Every finite-support repeating trace with drift \(\drift=(a,b)\) has \begin{equation}\label{eq:P16} \growth\ \ge\ 2\max(|a|,|b|). \end{equation} In particular \(\growth=0\) is impossible (an independent proof of Theorem 6.1). If a residue fiber contains exactly two strands, both bases share checkerboard parity.
Proof. A sum of \(m\) signs \(\pm1\) that is \(\equiv2\pmod4\) is nonzero with \(m\) positive and even; so each residue class mod \(|a|\) contains \(\ge2\) strand bases, giving \(\growth\ge2|a|\), and symmetrically \(\growth\ge2|b|\). If \(\growth=0\) some class is empty while \(\drift\neq0\), contradicting \(\eqref{eq:res-a}\). A two-strand fiber has \(\che\)-sum \(\pm2\), forcing equal signs.

Unlike the even-winding proof, Theorem 7.1 needs neither axis alignment nor the clean-packet reduction; it applies directly to every repeating trace.

8. Frozen-loop memory and exterior dynamics

The results so far exclude all zero-growth periodic behaviour and bound the wake of positive-growth highways, but do not force an arbitrary orbit to become periodic. The obstruction is nonlocal: two boundary strands visible in the same local window may be joined through arbitrarily distant explored territory, and that hidden connectivity decides whether a later toggle merges or splits loops (Theorem 4.1). The following lemmas isolate the memory carrier.

Theorem 8.1 (P14: frozen-loop trichotomy). Freeze the colouring before a step and let \(\Gamma_t\) be the frozen routing cycle carrying the ant. Toggling swaps the successors of the two opposite incoming strands: same cycle \(\Rightarrow\) split, different cycles \(\Rightarrow\) merge. If active-cycle lengths are eventually \(\le L\) and every reabsorbed non-pristine daughter has age \(\le A\), then the active cycle plus the \(\le A{+}1\) recent daughters form a finite packet modulo translation, which repeats and yields a translating periodic word of nonzero drift. Hence every finite-support orbit satisfies at least one of: (1) active frozen-cycle lengths are unbounded; (2) reabsorption ages of non-pristine shed cycles are unbounded; (3) the orbit is eventually a translating periodic word.

Combining Theorem 8.1 with the escape bound (a fixed \(D\)-cell connected domain cannot repeat a colouring, so the ant escapes in \(\le2^D\) steps [6]) sharpens (2): an unbounded-age daughter of length \(L\) forces exterior-side excursions visiting \(\ge\lceil\log_2 D\rceil\) distinct cells.

Theorem 8.2 (P17–P21: black-lifetime reductions). (a) (P17) Bounded black count forces unbounded reabsorption ages of non-pristine shed loops. (b) (P18) If every \(\turnR\) visit is followed by its next (necessarily \(\turnL\)) visit within a uniform time, a finite normalised visit-history determines the future; the orbit is eventually translating with \(\growth>0\). (c) (P19) Under active-loop bound \(L\), the partner loop at every later toggle is \(\le L\), and no later temporal path segment through distinct cells has more than \(L\) positions. (d) (P20) An \(\turnR\) visit leaves a fixed directed successor arc until the next \(\turnL\); under bound \(L\) the carrying frozen cycle is \(\le L\), and if each uninterrupted inactive episode lasts \(\le D\), the whole lifetime is a finite-state function of \(L+D\). (e) (P21) A sufficiently long \(\turnR\)-to-\(\turnL\) lifetime contains the ending time of another lifetime longer than any threshold \(A\); long black memory occurs in crossing-or-nested temporal clusters, not in isolation.
Theorem 8.3 (P23–P26: renewal flights and bounded islands). (P23) With memory horizon \(A\) chosen after all first encounters with \(B_0\), a warmed renewal flight has a finite-history renewal structure. (P24) If active Tait cycles are eventually bounded, every black Tait component has uniformly bounded size and diameter. (P25) Arbitrarily long bad seam episodes contain detached stationary seam islands of unbounded stationary duration and excursion radius. (P26) A warmed nonzero-drift renewal flight that moves its complete footprint beyond the finite black support present when that flight began can never be interrupted; it escapes forever as a positive-growth periodic trace.
Remark 8.4. Theorem 8.3 reduces any returning “bad core” to either an unbounded chronological chain of flight changes forced by older debris, or quiet intervals repeatedly too short to complete a warmed record cycle. Abstract planar binary-counter and one-marker returners show that locality, finite state, bounded components, finite support, a fixed anchor, and even injectivity on the reachable orbit do not by themselves exclude such behaviour. The unbounded-active-loop branch remains without any coercive invariant.

9. Collision-chain parity and lifetime interlacement

Encode a finite cell set \(D\) as edges of the bipartite row–column incidence graph: \((x,y)\) is the edge \(C_xR_y\). To a directed state \(s=(p,q)\) assign \(\eta(s)=C_{p_x}\) if \(q\) is vertical, \(R_{p_y}\) if horizontal.

Theorem 9.1 (P27: two-endpoint defect boundary). Over \(\F_2\), the boundary of the row–column incidence graph of \(B_s\triangle B_t\) equals \(\eta(s)+\eta(t)\). Intermediate tokens telescope across arbitrary collision checkpoints; hence at most one defect component is non-Eulerian, and a same-pose return has only Eulerian defect components.
Theorem 9.2 (P28, P29: collision dichotomy and four-cell minimum). At an \(\turnL\) collision the occupied Tait edge is a bridge exactly when the live and opposite roots lie on the same frozen boundary (deletion splits it); a nonbridge puts them on distinct boundaries (deletion merges them). Every completed \(\turnR\)-to-\(\turnL\) black lifetime has an Eulerian odd-visit component through its paired cell and therefore involves at least four distinct cells; in the four-cell case, signed mod-four charges determine the rectangle’s add/remove pattern from arrival type and side parities.

For the periodic branch, pair every stabilised \(\turnR\) with its following \(\turnL\) into a \(4\)-valent temporal vertex; wake \(\turnR\) events are degree-two \(H/V\) switches. Suppressing the switches gives a connected \(4\)-regular Euler system, and Traldi’s modified interlacement rows span the cycle space of the contracted touch graph [7],[8].

Theorem 9.3 (P31: contracted touch-graph rank). Let \(m\) be the number of paired lifetimes, \(c\) the number of closed \(H/V\) smoothing circuits, and \(\kappa\) the number of cycles in the \(2\)-regular wake-edge subgraph \(W\). The labelled boundary touch graph \(T\) has \(\dim Z(T)=m-c+1\) and its contraction \(T/W\) has \(\dim Z(T/W)=m-c-\kappa+1\), fitting the exact sequence \(0\to Z(W)\to Z(T)\to Z(T/W)\to0\). The lifetime rows span \(Z(T/W)\) but have codimension \(\le\kappa\) in \(Z(T)\). On the standard trace \(m=46,c=5,\kappa=1\), projected rank \(41\), full boundary rank \(42\).

Thus general circuit-nullity closes the contracted graph but cannot supply the missing \(\kappa\)-dimensional wake-cycle restriction; at fixed \(\growth=4\) there is an explicit abstract family with \(c\to\infty\) and unbounded contracted rank, so no bound depending only on \(\growth\) exists. Controlling that kernel through Langton-specific voltage/lattice data is the unresolved periodic step.

11. The central gap and open problems

The results above reduce Conjecture 1.1 to a single missing geometric statement.

Problem 11.1 (bounded-retreat lemma). For every finite-support initial configuration there exist a diagonal direction, a time \(T\), and a width \(W\lt\infty\) such that after \(T\) the ant never retreats more than \(W\) layers behind its current record frontier and all transverse excursions stay in a width-\(W\) corridor.
Problem 11.2 (bounded active core). The portion of the Tait graph belonging to the ant’s active boundary component and lying at or ahead of the current record has uniformly bounded size, and components shed behind an advancing frontier can no longer be reabsorbed.

Under either, the colours in a bounded frontier window, the ant’s position and heading form a finite state modulo translation; determinism forces eventual periodicity, and Theorem 3.1 classifies the cycle. No proof is known, and the following honest obstructions show why:

  • Unboundedness (Theorem 2.3) and fixed-domain escape do not imply bounded retreat: an orbit may keep enlarging a two-dimensional region while escaping every fixed box.
  • No single scalar Tait invariant is monotone (Remark 4.4).
  • A local pumping argument is invalid: equal local cores can be joined through different distant connectivity, changing a later merge into a split (Theorem 4.1).
  • At fixed growth \(\growth=4\) the signed-residue skeletons (Theorem 7.1) and touch-graph rank (Theorem 9.3) leave unbounded free parameters; six exact heading-reset countermodels satisfy every incidence/residue condition yet fail the cross-period criterion, so no incidence-only argument can finish the periodic branch.
Problem 11.3 (periodic classification). Prove that every positive-growth periodic trace admitted by finite support is the standard period-\(104\) trace up to symmetry and phase. This requires a single-tour interlacement/chronology theorem valid for unbounded period; the bounded-period exclusions of §10 are only a finite frontier.

A genuine disproof would output an infinite certificate: a nonstandard periodic word passing Theorem 3.1 (disproving uniqueness), or a finite self-enlarging Tait gadget whose state recurs with a parameter increased. No such certificate has been found.

12. Summary

We have assembled a coherent theory of the finite-support Langton ant: an exact decision criterion for periodic highways, a Tait-graph conjugacy with surgery calculus, two independent proofs that no zero-growth clean translator exists (hence every highway grows a wake of size \(\in4\Z_{>0}\)), the strand-density bound \(\growth\ge2\|\drift\|_\infty\), exhaustive exclusion of all periodic highways of period \(\le48\), and a precise reduction of the outstanding difficulty to a bounded-retreat/bounded-core lemma. The algebraic heart is machine-checked in Lean 4. Conjecture 1.1 remains open; we have delimited exactly what a solution must still supply.

Appendix A. Lean 4 formalization of the algebraic core

A dependency-light Lean 4 project (pinned to Lean \(4.32.0\), standard library only) machine-checks the exact transition kernel and the algebraic invariants above. All audited theorems depend only on the standard axioms propext, Classical.choice, Quot.sound; there are no sorrys, no custom axioms, and no native_decide. The checked statements include:

  • the finite-support transition kernel and its toggle lemmas (the departed cell flips; every other cell is unchanged);
  • the heading/black-count reset theorem (Proposition 2.4, mod 4);
  • the finite parity telescope evenWindingParityCore—the core \(\eqref{eq:telescope}\) of Theorem 6.1—and the contradiction wrapper noOddWindingFromFiniteHalfFlow;
  • the four-corner admissibility and mod-four fiber arithmetic of Theorem 7.1: \(E_r+2=0\Rightarrow E_r=2\) in \(\Z/4\), a residue fiber \(=2\) has a positive even number of bases, fibers partition the base list, and one identity per residue implies \(\growth\ge2n\);
  • finite potential telescoping, checker-cycle monodromy \(2\sum\alpha\), and translated-widget cancellation (the steps \(\eqref{eq:monodromy}\)–\(\eqref{eq:reduced}\));
  • collision-chain endpoint parity (Theorem 9.1) and exact directed-pose row/column \(\Z/4\) discrepancy charges (Theorem 9.2).

The formalization isolates, as an explicit interface, the endpoint normal form and lifted potential geometry of a genuine translator. Consequently neither Theorem 7.1/7.2 nor Conjecture 1.1 is claimed as an unconditional Lean theorem; what is certified is the algebraic layer conditional on a structured trace certificate, together with the growth bound it yields.
